The Belt and Road Initiative

Reshaping the Urban Network Across Eurasia

The Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), unveiled in 2013, takes its name from two historic trade routes: the overland “Silk Road Economic Belt” and the maritime “21st-Century Maritime Silk Road,” together aiming to revive and expand the ancient Silk Road network.

On May 25, 2024, the X8157 China-Europe freight train departed from Xi'an International Port Station and headed for Malaszewicze, Poland.

A cargo ship from China's Taicang Port arrives at Peru's giant deepwater port of Chancay

2023 marks the 10th anniversary of the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), which has seen over 150 countries and 30 international organizations join and sign more than 200 cooperation documents. Spanning Asia, Europe, Africa, and beyond, the BRI drives infrastructure development in railways, highways, ports, energy pipelines, and digital networks across its member nations.

China’s Belt and Road Initiative burst onto the world stage in October 2013 in Astana and Jakarta to unveil an overland “Silk Road Economic Belt” and a “21st-Century Maritime Silk Road.” Its inaugural memorandum with Kazakhstan set the pace, quickly followed by a pact in Indonesia. Within a year, scores of nations across Asia, Europe and Africa rallied behind BRI’s vision, pledging to modernize railways, power grids and digital networks.

By 2014, the newly formed Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank—born of BRI ambition—welcomed 21 founding members, from India to Italy. Today, the BRI spans hundreds of ventures worth over \$800 billion, weaving a vibrant tapestry of trade routes and forging fresh bonds across continents.

CR Express

Since its 2013 launch under the Belt and Road Initiative, the China Railway Express (CR Express) has become a vital overland artery. What began as service to just a few European cities now spans 82 routes across 25 countries and over 200 cities, with more than 190,000 trains operated to date. In 2024, rail freight volume reached 2.07 million TEU—over 80% of China–Europe land trade—while new models such as cold‐chain logistics and cross‐border e-commerce have emerged. This network solidifies the rail link as a cornerstone for Asia–Europe industrial collaboration, driving faster, more diversified supply chains across continents.
Source: China State Railway Group Co., Ltd.

The West Corridor links China’s interior to Europe via two major crossings, handling over 60% of China–Europe freight and mainly moving manufactured goods from the Yangtze and Pearl River regions.

The Middle Corridor runs through a northern gateway, connecting China’s heartland with Russia and the Nordics, specializing in fast transport of automotive and heavy machinery.

The East Corridor employs two northeastern crossings to bridge China’s old industrial base with Europe, facilitating Asian trade flows and accounting for 28% of corridor cargo in 2024.

Maritime Silk Road

As the CR Express flourished, the 21st-Century Maritime Silk Road simultaneously enhanced East - West sea networks. China partnered with coastal nations in Southeast Asia, South Asia, Africa, and Europe to upgrade port infrastructure and optimize routes—jointly managing operations at Piraeus in Greece and Gwadar in Pakistan. Investments in logistics hubs and expanded regional free-trade agreements have driven significant growth in maritime cargo over the past decade. Strengthened interconnectivity in energy and manufacturing, along with digital and green technologies in port development, have further reinforced the global impact of these maritime routes.

Visualization of Maritime Silk Road Ports

The Maritime Silk Road was an ancient maritime route for trade and cultural exchanges between China and foreign countries. It mainly consisted of the East China Sea route and the South China Sea route. It emerged during the Qin and Han dynasties, developed in the Three Kingdoms and Sui dynasties, reached its peak in the Tang and Song dynasties, and underwent changes in the Ming and Qing dynasties. It is known as one of the oldest maritime routes in the world.

This map shows the changes in the freight throughput (TEU) of major ports and the GDP of their respective cities from 2013 to 2023. The Maritime Silk Road not only promoted economic development but also facilitated the spread of cultures, technologies, and ideas between different regions. It connected the East and the West, enabling the exchange of goods such as silk, porcelain, spices, and precious metals. Through this route, merchants from various countries established trade relations, leading to the growth of port cities and the development of international trade networks. The cultural exchanges along the Maritime Silk Road also had a profound impact on art, religion, and science, contributing to the diversity and progress of human civilization.

Over the past three sections, we’ve followed the story of the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) through maps and movement — from its big-picture vision to the cities it connects. We began with a global view, tracing how China’s 2013 proposal quickly turned into a worldwide network of partnerships. Through this lens, we saw how infrastructure and diplomacy came together to reshape international connectivity.
Zooming in, we explored the China-Europe Railway Express, where trains now carry goods across thousands of kilometers, linking inland cities like Xi’an and Chongqing to European trade hubs. Then, through maritime routes, we followed the flow of ships between key ports in Asia, Africa, and Europe — showing how the Maritime Silk Road reinforces a global chain of logistics and commerce.
Altogether, the BRI isn’t just laying down tracks and building harbors. It’s creating a web of cities, supply chains, and shared development. These visualizations help us see how geography, policy, and economy interact — turning distant cities into connected players on the global stage. The future of globalization may not be one giant leap, but thousands of local connections — and we’re already watching them form.
